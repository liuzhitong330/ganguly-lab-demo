Thalamocortical Spindles, Corticostriatal Reversal, and GABA — The Circuit Substrate of Motor Consolidation

Cathy Liu · cathyliu014@gmail.com · personal exploratory project, July 2026
motor learning sleep consolidation thalamocortical spindles corticostriatal GABA stroke recovery Allen Brain Atlas Python

The Ganguly lab has established three interlinked circuit-level findings that I find compelling to think about together: sleep spindles coupled to slow oscillations drive motor memory consolidation; the direction of information flow between motor cortex and striatum reverses as skills become consolidated; and GABAergic signaling — disrupted by stroke — is required for the spindle-slow oscillation coupling that supports recovery. These three findings implicate three overlapping circuits: thalamocortical, corticostriatal, and local GABAergic networks in motor and prefrontal cortex.

I mapped the expression of genes encoding the key molecular players in each of these circuits across brain regions using the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas, asking where the molecular substrates for each circuit are concentrated and how they overlap.

What this is. Personal exploratory analysis, not novel research. Data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (Allen Institute for Brain Science, 2004), via the public RMA API. ISH expression energy is a bulk regional average. The gene-to-circuit mapping reflects established literature on each protein's function.

Regional landscape — all three circuits

Three circuits in detail

Thalamic Cacna1g (13.2) and motor cortex Hcn1 (7.1) anchor the spindle circuit. Cav3.1 (Cacna1g) T-type Ca2+ channels in the thalamic reticular nucleus generate the 12–15 Hz spindle bursts. Hcn1 (Ih current) in layer 5 motor cortex neurons sets the membrane time constant that determines how cortical neurons respond to thalamocortical spindle input — and how they rebound into the next slow oscillation cycle. The lab's finding that spindle coupling to slow oscillations is specifically disrupted by stroke, and that recovery of this coupling correlates with functional gains, maps directly onto this Cacna1g → Hcn1 axis: stroke presumably disrupts the thalamocortical interface, not the thalamic spindle generator itself, which would predict that interventions targeting Hcn1-mediated cortical rebound excitability could rescue consolidation.
Slc17a7 (VGLUT1) massive in PFC (37.6) and Drd1/Foxp1 concentrated in striatum (21.5 / 14.7). The corticostriatal projection — PFC glutamatergic axons (VGLUT1) synapsing on D1-expressing striatal MSNs (Foxp1+) — is the anatomical substrate for the information-flow reversal the lab identified in motor learning (Current Biology 2024). During early exploratory learning, information flows cortex → striatum (credit assignment to motor actions). As skills consolidate, the dominant flow reverses — striatal activity predicts cortical dynamics. The asymmetric expression of VGLUT1 in PFC vs DRD1/FOXP1 in striatum reflects the two ends of this directional loop, and the D1/D2 ratio (21.5 vs 10.9) suggests the direct pathway has a 2:1 expression advantage at this stage — consistent with reward-based consolidation favoring the direct pathway.
Gad1/Gad2 peak in striatum (13.4/14.6); Pvalb in motor cortex and midbrain; Sst in PFC. The lab's stroke-consolidation work showed that GABAergic signaling is specifically required for the recovery of spindle-slow oscillation coupling. Three GABAergic subpopulations are implicated in different layers of this: (1) striatal MSNs (Gad1/Gad2-expressing) gate corticostriatal output, (2) parvalbumin interneurons (Pvalb, high in motor cortex) generate the cortical gamma oscillations that nest within spindles, and (3) SST interneurons (Sst, high in PFC) modulate slow oscillation up-states. Each population is a candidate for why GABAergic disruption after stroke impairs the coordination across all three timescales simultaneously.

Why this connects to the lab's work

  • Motor learning and sleep: The thalamocortical spindle circuit (Cacna1g → Hcn1) and the corticostriatal reversal circuit (VGLUT1 → DRD1) are the two biological systems underlying the lab's main experimental questions. I'd like to contribute to experiments that manipulate these specific molecular nodes — optogenetically, pharmacologically, or via targeted stimulation — to test causal roles during sleep-dependent consolidation.
  • Stroke recovery: The GABAergic subpopulation map (PV, SST, striatal GAD1/2) suggests specific targets for restoring consolidation after stroke. I'm interested in contributing to the analysis of multi-area LFP recordings and to experiments that test whether restoring specific GABA subtype function accelerates recovery.
  • BCIs: The stability of neuroprosthetic control resembles natural skill consolidation, and the lab has shown that the same thalamocortical coupling dynamics are involved. I want to contribute to the neural data analysis pipeline for understanding how motor cortex representations stabilize during long-term BCI use.
  • My background spans wet lab molecular and cell biology and computational analysis of neural data. I think my combination of practical lab experience and computational background is where I can be most useful to your lab.
